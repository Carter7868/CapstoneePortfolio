Looking Back,
Moving Forward.

A first-person reflection on learning, growth, and what it means to engineer something that matters — through my Capstone and four years at UBC Okanagan.

--- / 300+ words
Calculating...

Engineering school is full of problems with known answers. Capstone was the first time the answer genuinely didn't exist yet — and that changed everything about how I think about what it means to be an engineer.

01
What did you learn about the design process from your experience in this course?

The most important thing I learned about the design process is that it is genuinely iterative — not just in theory, but in a way that you can only really appreciate by living through it. I came into Capstone thinking that good engineering meant choosing the right answer early and executing it cleanly. What I found was messier and more honest than that.

On the battery subsystem I was responsible for, I went through multiple casing designs before arriving at something that actually worked. Each iteration wasn't a failure — it was data. The thermal issues I discovered in early versions directly informed the airflow channel geometry in the final design. That progression only happened because I was willing to treat a version that didn't work as a result worth learning from, rather than a setback to move past as quickly as possible.

I also learned how much the design process depends on clearly defined requirements. When our objective — maximize flight time — was crisp and quantified, every design decision had a reference point. Ambiguous goals create ambiguous designs. That's a lesson I'll carry into every project I work on.

02
Based on your experiences in this course, what are the most important attributes of an engineer?

Technical knowledge matters — but I found that the engineers on our team who moved the project forward most effectively were the ones who combined technical competence with two other things: intellectual honesty and the ability to communicate clearly.

Intellectual honesty means being willing to say "this isn't working" before it's obvious to everyone else, and being genuinely open to feedback that challenges your design choices. I had to revise my battery casing multiple times, and each time it required letting go of something I'd spent real time on. That's hard. But the engineers who can't do that end up defending bad designs instead of improving them.

Communication matters because engineering doesn't happen in isolation. Our client, North Shore Rescue, needed to understand what we were building and why — not the circuit diagrams, but the decisions and the trade-offs. Being able to explain a thermal management problem to someone without an electrical background is genuinely a technical skill, and one I want to keep developing.

03
How has your experience on this project contributed to your understanding of the role and responsibilities of the professional engineer in society?

Working with North Shore Rescue made the stakes concrete in a way that coursework never quite does. These aren't abstract users — they're volunteers who go into the mountains to bring people home safely. The UAV we designed could, in a real deployment scenario, be the difference between a rescuer having radio contact and not having it. That is a real responsibility, and it sat differently than any lab report or assignment.

I came away with a much clearer sense of what professional accountability actually means. The P.Eng. designation isn't just a credential — it's a statement that you take responsibility for your work in the real world, where failures have consequences that extend beyond a grade. Throughout this project, I found myself making decisions I genuinely had to own: cell selection, thermal margins, casing geometry. If those had been wrong in the field, it would have mattered.

My broader time at UBC Okanagan, particularly coursework on engineering ethics and sustainability, gave me frameworks for thinking about the engineer's role in society. Capstone gave me the context to understand why those frameworks exist.

04
What strengths do you bring to your engineering career, and which areas need growth?

My strongest asset is hands-on fluency. I've been building things — drones, 3D printers, electronics — since I was a teenager, and that background means I approach engineering problems with a builder's intuition. I can hold a design in my head and already be thinking about how it fits together physically, what will be annoying to assemble, what will be hard to service. That's not something you get from lectures.

I'm also genuinely curious in a way that keeps me learning independently. The software list on my resume isn't from courses — most of it came from personal projects where I wanted to do something and taught myself what I needed to know to do it. That attitude will serve me well in any technical role where the tools are always evolving.

Where I need to grow is in formal project management and structured technical documentation. I'm comfortable with the making — I want to get better at the communicating. My plan is to seek roles early in my career where I work alongside experienced engineers whose documentation and communication habits I can learn from directly, while continuing to build personal projects that push the technical edge of what I know.
